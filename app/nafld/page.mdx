# Fatty Liver Disease and Insulin Resistance: What the Research Shows

If you've recently been told your liver has extra fat in it — or that you have NAFLD or MASLD — you're not alone, and you don't need to panic. This is one of the most common liver conditions in the world, affecting roughly one in four adults globally (CGH, 2025). It's also one of the conditions most closely tied to how your body handles insulin, which means the lifestyle levers that improve insulin sensitivity can directly benefit your liver.

This article walks through what the science actually says about fatty liver disease: why it happens, who gets it, what diet and exercise research shows, and what you can realistically do next.

## NAFLD vs MASLD — Why the Name Changed

You may have heard both "NAFLD" and "MASLD" used to describe the same condition and wondered which one is correct. The short answer: MASLD is the newer, preferred term — and the reason for the name change actually tells you something important about the disease itself.

For decades, doctors used the term **Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)** to describe excess fat accumulation in the liver not caused by heavy alcohol use. The problem with that name is that it defines the condition by what it *isn't* (not alcoholic) rather than what it *is*. It also carried an unintentional stigma, implying that the condition was simply a lifestyle failure.

In 2023, an international panel of liver specialists formally replaced NAFLD with **Metabolic dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease (MASLD)**. The new name correctly identifies the underlying driver: problems with metabolism, particularly insulin resistance, high blood sugar, high triglycerides, and related factors. The shift in language matters because it reflects a deeper scientific understanding: this is a metabolic disease, not simply a consequence of eating too much fat (CGH, 2025).

MASLD exists on a spectrum. At the mild end, there is simple steatosis — fat in the liver cells, but no significant inflammation or scarring. A subset of people develop **MASH (Metabolic dysfunction-Associated SteatoHepatitis)**, which involves liver cell injury and inflammation. A smaller fraction progress to fibrosis (scarring). The good news is that the early stages are often reversible with lifestyle change (CGH, 2025).

## The Insulin Resistance Connection

To understand why fat builds up in the liver, it helps to understand what insulin normally does — and what happens when cells stop responding to it properly.

Insulin is a hormone released by your pancreas after you eat. Its main job is to act like a key, unlocking your cells so they can absorb glucose from your bloodstream for energy. When cells become resistant to insulin, the key stops working efficiently. Your pancreas responds by producing more and more insulin. This state — high insulin levels in the blood, with cells that don't respond well — is called **insulin resistance (IR)**.

In the liver, insulin resistance has a specific and well-studied consequence. Normally, insulin signals the liver to stop producing glucose and to stop making new fat from scratch. When the liver becomes insulin resistant, it stops receiving that "stand down" signal properly. At the same time, the liver remains responsive to the fat-producing effects of insulin in certain pathways. The result is a paradox: the liver keeps running a process called **de novo lipogenesis** — literally "new fat creation" — turning excess carbohydrates into fat even when it shouldn't be (ScienceDirect, 2024).

<KeyInsight type="research">
This newly manufactured fat accumulates in the liver cells themselves. Meanwhile, the liver also receives more fat from the bloodstream, because insulin resistance in fat tissue causes fat cells to release fatty acids more readily. The liver ends up overwhelmed — taking in fat from multiple directions and struggling to export or burn it fast enough (ScienceDirect, 2024). This is why improving insulin sensitivity is considered central to treating MASLD.
</KeyInsight>

## Who Gets Fatty Liver Disease?

The short answer is: a much wider range of people than most assume.

The classic risk profile is someone with obesity, type 2 diabetes, or metabolic syndrome — and indeed, MASLD is more prevalent in those groups. Studies estimate that roughly 55–70% of people with type 2 diabetes have MASLD, compared to about 25% of the general adult population (CGH, 2025). High triglycerides, low HDL cholesterol, and high blood pressure are all independently associated with higher MASLD risk.

But there is an important caveat: **MASLD is not exclusively a disease of people with obesity**. Research on what is called "lean MASLD" has shown that approximately 20% of all MASLD cases occur in people with a normal BMI (Xiong et al., 2024). These individuals have normal body weight but still develop metabolic dysfunction — often due to insulin resistance driven by factors like genetics, visceral fat (fat stored around the organs rather than under the skin), sedentary behavior, or dietary patterns.

<KeyInsight type="important">
Lean MASLD is important to recognize because it challenges the assumption that "if your weight is normal, your liver is fine." The underlying drivers — insulin resistance and visceral adiposity — are the same regardless of body weight, meaning the same lifestyle strategies are relevant even for those at a healthy BMI (Xiong et al., 2024). If you have a normal weight, MASLD is still worth discussing with your doctor.
</KeyInsight>

Genetics also play a meaningful role. Certain gene variants, particularly in the PNPLA3 and TM6SF2 genes, significantly increase susceptibility to MASLD and faster progression to fibrosis. This explains why some people with severe obesity never develop significant liver disease while others with modest metabolic risk factors do (CGH, 2025).

Other risk factors include polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), hypothyroidism, sleep apnea, certain medications (including some corticosteroids and antipsychotics), and paradoxically, very rapid weight loss — which can temporarily worsen liver inflammation.

## What Diet Research Shows

Diet is one of the most studied interventions for MASLD, and the evidence points fairly consistently in a few directions.

### The Mediterranean Diet

Of all the dietary patterns studied for MASLD, the **Mediterranean diet** has the strongest and most consistent evidence base. Multiple observational studies and randomized controlled trials have shown that adherence to a Mediterranean-style eating pattern is associated with lower liver fat, reduced inflammation, and improvements in insulin sensitivity (Frontiers, 2025).

The Mediterranean diet emphasizes vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, nuts, olive oil as the primary fat, and fish — particularly fatty fish — while limiting red meat, processed foods, and added sugar. It is not a low-fat diet; the fat it includes comes primarily from extra-virgin olive oil and omega-3-rich fish, both of which have anti-inflammatory properties.

### Fructose and Processed Foods

Of individual dietary factors, **fructose** has received particular scientific scrutiny in the context of MASLD. Fructose, unlike glucose, is metabolized almost entirely in the liver. When fructose arrives at the liver in large amounts, it preferentially drives de novo lipogenesis — exactly the fat-creation process described above. High fructose intake has been linked to increased liver fat in both animal models and human studies (Frontiers, 2025).

The main sources of fructose in Western diets are **sugar-sweetened beverages** (sodas, fruit juices, sports drinks) and **ultra-processed foods** containing high-fructose corn syrup or added sucrose. Research shows that people with high consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages have significantly higher rates of MASLD, even after controlling for total calorie intake (Frontiers, 2025).

Practical dietary priorities supported by research:

- Replace sugar-sweetened drinks with water, sparkling water, or unsweetened tea
- Reduce or eliminate processed snack foods, fast food, and packaged sweets
- Increase vegetables, especially non-starchy ones
- Choose whole grains over refined grains
- Use olive oil as your primary cooking fat
- Eat fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) two to three times per week

## What Exercise Does to Fatty Liver

One of the most encouraging findings in MASLD research is that **exercise reduces liver fat even when it doesn't cause significant weight loss**. This is important because it means you don't have to achieve a specific number on the scale to benefit your liver — movement itself has direct therapeutic effects.

A systematic review and meta-analysis by Haigh et al. (2022) examined exercise interventions across multiple randomized controlled trials and found consistent, statistically significant reductions in liver fat content — measured by MRI and liver biopsy — in exercise groups compared to controls. Crucially, these reductions occurred even in trials where body weight did not significantly change.

Both **aerobic exercise** and **resistance (strength) training** reduced liver fat in the studies analyzed. Aerobic exercise — brisk walking, cycling, jogging, swimming — tended to show slightly larger effects on liver fat reduction, but resistance training also produced meaningful improvements and had additional benefits for insulin sensitivity and muscle mass (Haigh et al., 2022).

<KeyInsight type="action">
**Current evidence suggests at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise**, combined with two or more days per week of resistance training, is a reasonable target for people with MASLD. However, Haigh et al. (2022) found meaningful benefits even with lower volumes — particularly in previously sedentary individuals where any increase in activity produced measurable improvements. Starting somewhere matters more than starting perfectly.
</KeyInsight>

The mechanisms are multiple: exercise increases the liver's ability to export and oxidize fat; muscle contraction during exercise draws glucose out of the bloodstream, reducing the load the liver has to process; regular exercise improves whole-body insulin sensitivity, reducing the hyperinsulinemia that drives de novo lipogenesis; and exercise reduces visceral fat — the metabolically active fat stored around organs — which is a key driver of liver fat accumulation.

## Questions to Bring to Your Doctor

1. **What stage of fatty liver disease do I have?** Understanding whether you have simple steatosis or more advanced MASH/fibrosis helps calibrate how urgently you need to act and what monitoring is appropriate.

2. **Do I need additional tests to assess fibrosis?** Non-invasive tests like FIB-4 score, liver elastography (fibroscan), or enhanced liver fibrosis (ELF) test can estimate the degree of liver scarring without a biopsy.

3. **Should I be screened for type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease?** MASLD is closely linked to both conditions. If you haven't been recently screened, it's worth asking.

4. **Are there any medications I'm currently taking that might be affecting my liver?** Some common medications — including certain antidepressants, corticosteroids, and antipsychotics — can contribute to liver fat accumulation.

5. **What does a realistic improvement timeline look like for my situation?** Studies show liver fat can begin to decrease within weeks of dietary and exercise changes, but this varies by individual.

6. **Would a referral to a hepatologist, dietitian, or endocrinologist be appropriate?** For more advanced disease, or if you're struggling with lifestyle changes on your own, specialist support can make a significant difference.

7. **How will we monitor progress?** Ask about follow-up imaging, liver enzyme tests (ALT, AST), and metabolic markers so you have concrete ways to track improvement over time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQItem question="Can fatty liver disease be reversed?">

The early stages of MASLD — simple steatosis and mild MASH — can often show significant improvement with sustained lifestyle changes, including diet modification and regular exercise. Studies have documented reductions in liver fat content and normalization of liver enzymes in patients who make consistent changes. "Reversal" is a term used cautiously by researchers because the degree of improvement varies by individual, stage of disease, and genetic factors. The important point is that meaningful improvement is well within the reach of many people, particularly at early stages (CGH, 2025). More advanced fibrosis is harder to reverse but can often be stabilized.

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Do I need to avoid all fats if I have fatty liver?">

No — and in fact, avoiding all fat would be counterproductive. The Mediterranean diet, which has the best evidence for MASLD, is not a low-fat diet. The key distinction is between the types of fat. Saturated fats (from red meat and processed foods) and trans fats appear harmful, while monounsaturated fats (from olive oil) and polyunsaturated omega-3 fats (from fatty fish and flaxseed) are associated with reduced liver inflammation and improved metabolic outcomes (Frontiers, 2025).

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="If I have a normal body weight, could I still have fatty liver?">

Yes. Lean MASLD — fatty liver in people with normal BMI — accounts for approximately 20% of all MASLD cases. Insulin resistance can develop in people of any body size, particularly in those with high visceral fat, sedentary lifestyles, poor dietary patterns, or certain genetic predispositions (Xiong et al., 2024). If you have been diagnosed with MASLD at a normal weight, the same lifestyle interventions that help people with obesity — diet improvement, exercise, reducing processed foods — are still directly relevant.

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How much exercise is needed to see liver benefits?">

Research suggests that both aerobic and resistance training reduce liver fat content, and that benefits can appear even without significant weight loss. A systematic review by Haigh et al. (2022) found improvements across a range of exercise volumes, with the general guidance pointing to at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity exercise as a meaningful target. However, any increase in activity from a sedentary baseline produces measurable benefits — you don't have to reach a specific threshold before your liver starts to respond.

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Are there medications approved specifically for MASLD?">

As of 2025, the treatment landscape is evolving. Resmetirom (brand name Rezdiffra) was approved by the FDA in 2024 as the first drug specifically indicated for MASH with liver fibrosis — a significant development for patients with more advanced disease. However, for most people with early-to-moderate MASLD, lifestyle intervention remains the cornerstone of management. Medication options are best discussed with a hepatologist or gastroenterologist who can assess your specific situation (CGH, 2025).

</FAQItem>

# What Is Prediabetes? Understanding Your Numbers and Your Options

You went in for a routine blood test — maybe a yearly physical, maybe a checkup after feeling a little off — and the doctor mentioned that your blood sugar was "a little high" or "borderline." Perhaps they used the word "prediabetes." You may have left the office with a printout, a few vague instructions about "watching what you eat," and a low-grade sense of dread that has been humming in the background ever since.

That anxiety is completely understandable. But here is what the research actually shows: prediabetes is not a verdict. It is information — early information — that gives you a genuine window to act before blood sugar regulation breaks down further. The evidence for that window is not wishful thinking. It comes from one of the largest and most rigorous prevention trials ever conducted (Knowler et al., 2002).

## Prediabetes vs Type 2 Diabetes — What's Actually Different?

Blood sugar regulation exists on a spectrum, not as a simple on/off switch. Prediabetes sits in the middle of that spectrum. The body is still regulating blood sugar, but not as cleanly as before. There are two distinct physiological patterns that can create a prediabetes reading:

- **Impaired Fasting Glucose (IFG):** Blood sugar is elevated after an overnight fast, suggesting the liver is releasing more glucose than it should when you have not eaten.
- **Impaired Glucose Tolerance (IGT):** Blood sugar spikes higher than normal after eating and does not return to baseline quickly enough. This reflects reduced sensitivity to insulin in muscle tissue.

Some people have one pattern; some have both. Both can show up in the HbA1c range of **5.7–6.4%** (NIH, 2025), which measures the average percentage of hemoglobin in your blood that has glucose attached to it — a snapshot of roughly the past two to three months of blood sugar levels.

<KeyInsight type="important">
**Prediabetes does not automatically progress to type 2 diabetes.** The annual rate of progression from prediabetes to T2DM is approximately 5–10%, and a meaningful proportion of people with prediabetes actually return to normal glucose levels over time — a process called regression to normoglycemia (JCEM, 2025). The direction your numbers move depends heavily on the choices and circumstances that influence insulin sensitivity, several of which are genuinely modifiable.
</KeyInsight>

## The Numbers Explained

Lab results can look like alphabet soup. Here is a plain-English breakdown of the three tests most commonly used to diagnose prediabetes.

### Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG)

This test measures the glucose concentration in your blood after you have fasted for at least eight hours. It reflects your baseline blood sugar with no food-related insulin response in play.

| Category | Fasting Glucose (mg/dL) |
|---|---|
| Normal | Below 100 |
| Prediabetes (IFG) | 100–125 |
| Diabetes | 126 or above |

A single reading in the prediabetes range on a fasting test is enough for a clinical flag, though most guidelines recommend confirming with a second test on a different day before acting on the number (NIH, 2025).

### Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)

Rather than capturing a single moment in time, HbA1c reflects roughly three months of average blood sugar exposure. Glucose in the bloodstream gradually attaches to hemoglobin molecules; the higher your blood sugar has been over that window, the higher the percentage.

| Category | HbA1c |
|---|---|
| Normal | Below 5.7% |
| Prediabetes | 5.7–6.4% |
| Diabetes | 6.5% or above |

The test is convenient because it does not require fasting and captures trends rather than single snapshots (NIH, 2025).

### Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)

The OGTT is considered the most sensitive diagnostic tool. You fast overnight, drink a standard glucose solution (75 grams), and have your blood drawn two hours later.

| Category | 2-Hour Post-Load Glucose (mg/dL) |
|---|---|
| Normal | Below 140 |
| Prediabetes (IGT) | 140–199 |
| Diabetes | 200 or above |

A reading in the 140–199 range indicates that your muscles and other tissues are not pulling glucose out of the bloodstream as efficiently as they should (NIH, 2025).

## The DPP Study — The Most Important Research You Haven't Heard Of

In 2002, the New England Journal of Medicine published results from the Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP), a randomized controlled trial that remains one of the most significant pieces of public health research of the past three decades.

The DPP enrolled **3,234 participants** with confirmed impaired glucose tolerance — meaning they were in the prediabetes zone. The average age was **51 years**. They were randomly assigned to: an intensive lifestyle intervention program, metformin, or a placebo.

The results after roughly three years were striking (Knowler et al., 2002):

- **Lifestyle intervention reduced the incidence of type 2 diabetes by 58%** compared to placebo.
- **Metformin reduced incidence by 31%** compared to placebo.
- Lifestyle intervention outperformed medication even in participants who were older or had higher baseline blood sugar.

<KeyInsight type="research">
What did the lifestyle intervention actually involve? It was not a crash diet or extreme fitness. The two primary targets were: **(1) at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity physical activity** — primarily brisk walking, averaging about 22 minutes per day — and **(2) a 7% reduction in body weight** for those who were overweight. Participants worked with lifestyle coaches who helped with practical goal-setting and gradual behavior change. The program was not about perfection; it was about consistent, moderate shifts (Knowler et al., 2002).
</KeyInsight>

The DPP findings have since been replicated in multiple countries and populations, including long-term follow-up data showing that the benefits of early lifestyle intervention persist for years after the active program ends.

## What Lifestyle Changes Actually Help

### Physical Activity: 150 Minutes per Week Is the Benchmark

The 150-minute weekly target from the DPP is the same recommendation you will find in major clinical guidelines. "Moderate intensity" means activity where you can talk but singing would be difficult — brisk walking, cycling on flat ground, swimming at a steady pace, dancing. You do not need a gym membership or specialized equipment.

Breaking it into smaller chunks works. Three 10-minute walks spread through the day produce measurable glycemic benefits. A short 10–15 minute walk **after eating** has been shown to blunt the blood sugar rise that typically follows a meal more effectively than a single longer walk taken at a different time of day (Amer et al., 2024).

Resistance training — bodyweight exercises, resistance bands, or weight training — also improves insulin sensitivity through different mechanisms than aerobic activity, and combining both types produces better results than either alone (Amer et al., 2024).

### Food: Reducing the Blood Sugar Load Without Obsessing Over Perfection

No single diet has been proven universally superior for prediabetes management, but certain patterns consistently show up in the evidence:

- **Reducing refined carbohydrates and ultra-processed foods** has a direct impact on post-meal blood sugar spikes and insulin demand.
- **Prioritizing fiber-rich foods** — vegetables, legumes, whole grains, berries — slows glucose absorption and feeds gut bacteria that influence metabolic health.
- **Including adequate protein** at meals helps with satiety and has a minimal effect on blood sugar compared to refined carbohydrates.
- **Reducing sugar-sweetened beverages** is one of the highest-impact single changes, because liquid calories with high sugar content are absorbed rapidly and bypass many of the satiety signals that solid food triggers.

These are not moral imperatives or reasons to feel shame about past eating patterns. Blood sugar dysregulation develops from a complex combination of genetics, stress, sleep quality, built environment, and economic access to food — not from personal failure.

### Sleep and Stress

Both have direct, documented effects on insulin sensitivity. Poor sleep — both insufficient duration and fragmented sleep — raises cortisol and growth hormone in ways that push blood sugar upward. Chronic psychological stress does the same through similar hormonal pathways.

### Weight: A Modest Reduction Can Matter

The DPP's 7% weight loss target is often misread as a minimum bar that is impossible to reach. Even smaller reductions — 3–5% of body weight — produce measurable improvements in insulin sensitivity and fasting glucose for many people. And weight is only one of several levers; people in the DPP who did not achieve the weight loss goal but **did** meet the exercise goal still significantly reduced their diabetes risk (Knowler et al., 2002).

## How Exercise Directly Lowers Blood Sugar

Understanding the mechanism can help make exercise feel less abstract and more motivating.

Normally, insulin acts as a "key" that unlocks muscle cells, allowing glucose to enter from the bloodstream. In prediabetes, the locks are stiff — the cells are insulin resistant, meaning it takes more insulin than usual to achieve the same glucose uptake. This is the core problem.

Exercise bypasses the lock entirely. During physical activity, muscle contractions trigger a protein called **GLUT4** to move from inside the cell to the cell membrane. GLUT4 is a glucose transporter — when it is on the surface, glucose from the bloodstream flows into the muscle cell without needing insulin at all. This is called **non-insulin-dependent glucose uptake**, and it happens even in cells that have become insulin resistant (PMC, 2021).

<KeyInsight type="action">
**A single bout of exercise lowers blood sugar for hours afterward — regardless of how insulin-sensitive your cells currently are.** You do not have to "fix" insulin resistance before exercise starts working. Beyond the acute effect, regular exercise over weeks and months triggers adaptations that improve long-term insulin sensitivity through increased muscle mass, upregulation of GLUT4 expression, and improvements in mitochondrial function (PMC, 2021).
</KeyInsight>

## Questions to Bring to Your Doctor

- **"What were my exact numbers, and where do they fall in the range?"** Understanding whether you are at the low or high end of the prediabetes range is relevant for how urgently to act.
- **"Should I get retested in six months, and which test will you use?"** Tracking direction over time matters more than any single reading.
- **"Would it help to see a registered dietitian?"** A dietitian with experience in metabolic health can translate general guidelines into a realistic plan for your specific life.
- **"Am I a candidate for a structured diabetes prevention program?"** The CDC recognizes the DPP program, and many versions are covered by Medicare and some private insurers for people with confirmed prediabetes.
- **"Should we discuss metformin?"** For some people — particularly those with higher HbA1c, a strong family history of T2DM, or who have difficulty sustaining lifestyle changes — metformin is a reasonable option that significantly reduces risk (Knowler et al., 2002).
- **"Are there other risk factors I should know about?"** Conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), sleep apnea, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease are independently associated with insulin resistance and may warrant separate attention.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQItem question="If I have prediabetes, will I definitely develop type 2 diabetes?">

No. The annual rate of progression from prediabetes to type 2 diabetes is roughly 5–10%, and a significant proportion of people with prediabetes return to normal blood sugar levels over time (JCEM, 2025). Progression is not inevitable. The landmark DPP study found that lifestyle intervention reduced the incidence of type 2 diabetes by 58% in people with confirmed impaired glucose tolerance — people who were, by definition, in the prediabetes range (Knowler et al., 2002).

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Can I reverse prediabetes?">

The term "reversal" is medically imprecise, but regression to normoglycemia — meaning blood sugar returns to the normal range — is documented and occurs in a meaningful percentage of people with prediabetes (JCEM, 2025). What the evidence supports is that lifestyle changes can move numbers in a favorable direction, and in many cases bring them back below diagnostic thresholds. Whether that is best described as "reversing" prediabetes or "managing glucose levels back into the normal range" is largely a semantic question.

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="How quickly can lifestyle changes affect my HbA1c?">

HbA1c reflects roughly three months of blood sugar exposure, so changes in behavior typically take at least three months to show up in the number. In the DPP and subsequent studies, meaningful reductions in HbA1c and fasting glucose were observed within three to six months of consistent lifestyle changes (Knowler et al., 2002; Amer et al., 2024). Fasting glucose can change more quickly — sometimes within weeks — because it is more sensitive to day-to-day habits.

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Do I need to cut out all carbohydrates?">

No. The evidence does not support complete carbohydrate elimination as necessary or superior for most people with prediabetes. What consistently shows benefit is reducing refined carbohydrates (white flour, added sugars, sugary beverages) and replacing them with fiber-rich whole foods — vegetables, legumes, whole grains, and fruit — which produce slower, more moderate blood sugar responses. Extremely low-carbohydrate diets can reduce blood sugar in the short term, but long-term adherence is a significant challenge, and outcomes are similar to other approaches when adherence is matched (JCEM, 2025).

</FAQItem>

<FAQItem question="Approximately how many US adults have prediabetes?">

According to current estimates, approximately **96 million US adults** — more than one in three — have prediabetes. The majority are unaware of it because the condition typically produces no symptoms (NIH, 2025). This makes routine screening important, particularly for adults over 35 who are overweight or have other risk factors such as a family history of type 2 diabetes, a history of gestational diabetes, or a diagnosis of PCOS or metabolic syndrome.

</FAQItem>
